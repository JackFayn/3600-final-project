How much CO2 will U.S. data centers put out by 2030?

We fit exponential curves to state-level MW capacity and grid emission intensity, then multiply them to estimate yearly pounds of CO2 through 2030. Five states, six years of EPA data, and one pretty alarming forecast.

How the math works

We have two moving pieces: how much power data centers draw, and how dirty that power is. Both are trending, so we fit each with its own exponential and then multiply. No hand-waving — here's every step.

1. Capacity fit

MW nameplate data (2021–2024 plus a 2030 industry target) is fit to:

MW(t) = a · eb(t−2021)

using SciPy's curve_fit (nonlinear least squares). We anchor the exponent at 2021 so a is interpretable as the 2021 baseline capacity.

2. Emission-intensity fit

EPA eGRID state totals for 2018–2023 are fit to:

E(t) = a · eb(t−2018)

Anchored at 2018 with an initial guess of p0 = (E2018, −0.1). The negative seed matters — without it the solver sometimes lands on a flat fit for states that are decarbonizing quickly (looking at you, Illinois).

3. Annualizing

MW is a rate. To get an annual energy figure we multiply by 8,760 hours per year (assuming continuous operation, which is basically true for data centers):

MWh(t) = MW(t) · 8760

4. Total emissions

Then it's just energy times dirtiness:

Total CO2(t)  =  aMW · ebMW(t−2021)  ·  8760  ·  aE · ebE(t−2018)

Because both pieces are exponentials, the product is also exponential — its growth rate is the sum of the two rates. For Virginia that sum is positive and big. For most other states the grid is cleaning up fast enough to cancel part of the capacity blow-up.

5. Fitted parameters

These are what curve_fit actually returned for each state:

State aMW bMW aE bE

6. Caveats we know about

  • Five data points for MW and six for emissions is thin. Confidence intervals on b are wide.
  • We assume 100% utilization. Real PUE > 1 and variable load mean our numbers are a rough upper bound.
  • Exponential extrapolation is optimistic in both directions — grid cleanup can't go below zero, and capacity will eventually saturate.
  • The 2030 MW point is an industry projection, not measured. It's pulling the fit a lot.
